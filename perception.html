Perception

Edges, contrast and some dots

How do we perceive our environment? Think of our perception as a frame that moves infront of a background. It constantly scans for figures on a background that seem most relevant to us.

Interesting is, that our perception leads this frame according to certain mechanisms. One of these mechanisms is, that our attention is looking for edges. Edges with clear contrast are easier to grasp and seperated from the background. Due to we are in UX, let’s make a graphic example. A dot can be found in the following box.

Do you see the dot? It is there. Promised. If you click on it you will win a pot of gold that is waiting at the foot of a rainbow for you. Ok, I am not serious here. You can not see that, because the dot has the same colour than the background. That means, yup, I inserted a 25x25 px dot in the upper right corner no one can actually see (smiling sweat). Can you see the dot in the next box?

This dot may be more visible to you, because it has a clear contrast in colour and therefore has a perceivable edge. Why you should keep on reading you ask? Well, we can add more dots if you prefer. Let’s add more dots. By the way, without counting, how many dots do you see in the box below?

Before you get the wrong idea, this is not a test. Our short term memory defines the amount of elements we can visually process at once. There are actually 5 dots inside the box above. What about now? How many dots are in the following box?

Have you been able to tell right away or did you have to count? Though, we can easily grasp 5 elements, most people can not instantly distinct the number of dots to be 6. They have to count. But what happens when we order the elements like in the next example?

Now the number is quicker to scan. Why? Our mind groups together two rows of threes. Also we have learned this formation of dots to be a symbol.That way our mind refers the information it perceives with information learned previously. Now this dice also displays some other parameter that make the difference whether we can perceive something easily or not. These parameters are: Proximity, Contiguity and Similarity. The more these elemets share those properties, the better they can be grouped.


Groups

Let’s move up to higher levels of the perception and memory. Elements can be grouped on all levels of the process. Elements in the beginning and at the end of a group stick better with the user. This is called ‘PIMACY’ and ‘RECENCY’. Let’s move away from the dot examples to be more practical.


Imagine you want to get a message across. Let’s make the message to be 16 sentences long. 4 sentences of introduction, 8 sentences main section, 4 sentences conlusion, as boringly but efficiently illustrated below.

To understand the effect, you have to know the following: Our brain is always wired to seek for orientation. The less ambiguous the information is, the better for recall.

Primacy: The first sentence is not only the first of all other sentences, but also the first of the introduction and the first of the whole message. It is the first on all levels.

Therefore it shares 3 edges (purple). This is easy to process for our mind, due to the unambiguaty of the location of information. The 5th sentence, however, has only 2 edges (orange) and has less properties for orientation. Remember, we are always seeking for orientation.

Recency: This is the same effect, only with the last element of a bulk of information. Studies have indeed shown, that content in the middle of a group of information is most vulnerable for interferences and mistakes.

I don’t want to go into detail how powerfull this knowledge is for marketing purposes. But one hint. There is one really bad area for CTAs. Also the whole group should be easy to scan for orientation purposes.
